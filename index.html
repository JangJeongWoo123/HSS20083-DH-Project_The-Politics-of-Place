About This Project

Jeongwoo Jang · KAIST HSS/DHS283 · 2026 Spring

Research Question & Answer

Why are the world's major space launch sites located where they are, and what does their spatial distribution reveal about the relationship between technology, geography, and power?

The evidence across 33 sites points to a consistent answer: launch sites are disproportionately located in places that states could use without significant political resistance — former colonial territories, indigenous lands, remote deserts, depopulated peripheries. This is not accidental. It reflects structural patterns in how states access land, and which communities have historically had the power to refuse.

Why This Project Matters

Space launch sites are usually discussed in terms of engineering. But when you look at where the world's major spaceports are actually located, a different pattern emerges. The geography of space infrastructure reflects not only engineering logic but also the political history of which lands and populations states have treated as available, expendable, or controllable.

This matters because it changes how we understand space exploration. If we treat launch sites as neutral technical choices, we miss the way that technological progress has repeatedly been built on the dispossession or marginalization of specific communities.

Key Terms Explained

Penal colony — A remote territory used by a colonial power to imprison and exile convicted criminals, often far from the metropolitan center. France operated penal colonies in French Guiana (including the infamous Devil's Island) from 1852 to 1953. The same territory later became the site of the Kourou space center.
Gulag — The Soviet system of forced labor camps, operational from the 1930s to the 1950s, in which millions of prisoners were used as a labor force for major infrastructure projects. Baikonur Cosmodrome was constructed in large part by military conscripts working under conditions similar to those of the Gulag era.
Quilombola — Communities descended from escaped enslaved Africans who established autonomous settlements (quilombos) in Brazil during the colonial period. Quilombola communities near Alcântara, Maranhão, had farmed the land for centuries before being displaced to make way for Brazil's space launch center.
Yi (彝) people — One of China's recognized ethnic minorities, with a distinct language, culture, and history, predominantly located in Sichuan, Yunnan, and Guizhou provinces. The Xichang Satellite Launch Center was built in Yi territory without meaningful consultation with the local community.
Sámi people — The indigenous people of Scandinavia (Sápmi), with traditional territories spanning northern Norway, Sweden, Finland, and Russia. The Esrange Space Center in Swedish Lapland is located in Sámi territory.

Category Framework

Military Origin — Sites originating from Cold War missile programs or active military installations. Engineering and strategic purposes were inseparable.
Colonial Territory — Sites built on land controlled through colonial or imperial relationships, where the host population had limited ability to refuse.
National Prestige — Sites built primarily as symbols of national technological ambition, often in domestic peripheries.
Commercial — Sites operated primarily by private companies, often inheriting military infrastructure or seeking low-regulation, low-cost environments.
Peripheral Land — Sites on marginal, depopulated, or indigenous territory chosen because the state could use it without significant political resistance.

Scholars & Further Reading

Peter Redfield
Space in the Tropics: From Convicts to Rockets in French Guiana (University of California Press, 2000)
The primary humanistic framework for this project. Redfield traces how French Guiana — a territory that served as a site of colonial punishment — was transformed into a high-tech space launch facility, arguing that space infrastructure does not transcend political history but is built on top of it. His analysis of Kourou provides the core interpretive model: colonial land relationships directly shape the placement of space infrastructure, and that relationship persists even after formal decolonization.
Immanuel Wallerstein (1930–2019)
The Modern World-System (Academic Press, 1974)
Sociologist and historical social scientist who developed World-Systems Theory. Wallerstein argued that the global economy operates through a structural division between core states — which command advanced technology and capital — and peripheral regions, which are systematically made available for activities that core states cannot or will not conduct near their own population centers. This framework explains why launch sites, across different countries, eras, and operators, so consistently appear in remote, sparsely inhabited, or politically subordinate locations.
Asif Azam Siddiqi
Challenge to Apollo: The Soviet Union and the Space Race, 1945–1974 (NASA, 2000); The Red Rockets' Glare: Spaceflight and the Soviet Imagination (Cambridge UP, 2010)
A leading historian of the Soviet space program. Siddiqi's work documents how Soviet space infrastructure — including Baikonur and Kapustin Yar — was built within the context of Cold War military strategy, state secrecy, and forced labor. His research is essential for understanding the military origins of Russian launch geography.
Hyoung Joon An (안형준)
National Aspirations, Imagined Futures, and Space Exploration: the Origin and Development of Korean Space Program 1958–2013 (PhD dissertation, Georgia Institute of Technology, 2015)
The definitive scholarly history of South Korea's space program in English. An traces how South Korea's space ambitions were driven by four successive rationales: modernization (1950s–60s), self-defense (1970–84), economic security (1985–97), and national prestige (1998–2013). His analysis directly informs this project's classification of Naro Space Center as a "National Prestige" site, and demonstrates why the location in Jeolla Province — historically marginalized in South Korean politics — reflects the same center-periphery dynamic visible across the global dataset.

Conclusion

What the Map Argues

The Pattern

Across the thirty-three sites examined in this project, a consistent pattern emerges: launch sites are disproportionately located in places that states could use without significant political resistance. Deserts, remote islands, former colonial territories, indigenous lands, depopulated peripheries — these are not incidentally where rockets launch from. They are where rockets launch from because states found them available.

Three Key Findings

1. Military infrastructure became space infrastructure. The majority of major launch sites originated as military missile facilities during the Cold War. Kapustin Yar (1946) was built using captured German V-2 technology and engineers. Baikonur (1955) was selected for its remoteness from Soviet population centers and foreign surveillance, constructed by military conscripts. Kennedy Space Center (1962) was built on a military test range that had operated since 1949. The space age did not begin from scratch — it began from a military geography already in place, and civilian and commercial programs inherited that geography rather than choosing it freely.

2. Colonial and peripheral territories bear a disproportionate share of the burden. Peter Redfield's analysis of Kourou shows the pattern most clearly: the same territory in French Guiana that served as a site of colonial punishment (Devil's Island penal colony, 1852–1953) became France's primary launch facility after France lost its Algerian site at Hammaguir when Algeria achieved independence in 1962. France did not abandon colonial geography — it relocated it. Alcântara in Brazil tells a similar story: land farmed for centuries by Quilombola communities was seized for a space program whose benefits flowed to the Brazilian state and its international partners, not to the displaced communities. In each case — Kourou, Alcântara, Woomera, Hammaguir, Luigi Broglio off the Kenyan coast — a region with less political power hosts infrastructure that serves national or supranational ambitions.

3. The commercial turn in space has not changed this pattern. SpaceX's Starbase in Boca Chica, Texas was built by displacing a small community near the US-Mexico border, one of the poorest regions in the United States. Spaceport America was funded by $220 million in public money from New Mexico — one of the poorest US states — for an industry whose benefits have been slow to reach the local economy. Rocket Lab's Māhia Peninsula facility was chosen for its apparent emptiness, a perception that ignores the ongoing presence of Māori communities. Commercial space has largely inherited or replicated the same geographic logic as its military and colonial predecessors.

What This Project Cannot Show

A map can show where launch sites are located. It cannot show the labor conditions of the workers who built them, the environmental consequences for surrounding ecosystems, the experiences of displaced communities, or the full complexity of political negotiations that preceded each site's construction. The interpretive notes are starting points, not conclusions.

A Final Thought

The space age is often narrated as humanity's escape from earthly constraints. This project insists that the infrastructure of transcendence is built on earth, in specific places, under specific political conditions. The geography of space is not neutral. It never was.

Data & Method

Transparency, Sources, and Interpretive Choices

Dataset Overview

This project examines 33 major operational or historically significant spaceports selected from a broader list of globally active launch facilities. The dataset was constructed manually. Sites were selected to represent geographic diversity (all inhabited continents), operator diversity (state, military, intergovernmental, commercial), and historical diversity (1946 to 2023). Colonial Territory and Commercial categories were intentionally expanded to reflect their global significance.

The complete structured dataset — including all 33 sites with coordinates, founding year, operator, category, geographic context, and tags — is available as an Excel spreadsheet submitted as part of the course assignment packet.

Category Construction and Theoretical Basis

The five categories were developed inductively after the initial dataset was assembled — categorization followed observation, not the reverse. I first compiled a neutral list of operational spaceports, and only then applied analytical frameworks to identify the patterns that emerged.

Peter Redfield's Space in the Tropics (2000) provides the theoretical model for the Colonial Territory category, demonstrating how colonial land relationships directly shape the placement of high-tech infrastructure.

Immanuel Wallerstein's World-Systems Theory (The Modern World-System, 1974) provides the basis for the Peripheral Land and Military Origin categories: core states command technology and capital; peripheral regions are made available for infrastructure that serves core interests. This explains why launch sites so consistently appear in remote or politically subordinate locations, regardless of era or operator type.
