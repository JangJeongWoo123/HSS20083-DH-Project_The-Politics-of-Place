The geography of space infrastructure is not only an engineering problem — it is a political one.

About This Project

Jeongwoo Jang · KAIST HSS/DHS283 · 2026 Spring

Why This Project Matters

Space launch sites are usually discussed in terms of engineering. But when you look at where the world's major spaceports are actually located — a former French penal colony in South America, the Kazakh steppe built by Gulag labor, a Brazilian coastal strip that displaced Quilombola communities, a desert on Yi ethnic minority land in Sichuan — a different pattern emerges. The geography of space infrastructure reflects not only engineering logic but also the political history of which lands and populations states have treated as available, expendable, or controllable.

This matters because it changes how we understand space exploration. If we treat launch sites as neutral technical choices, we miss the way that technological progress has repeatedly been built on the dispossession or marginalization of specific communities. For humanities scholars, this project demonstrates that technological infrastructure is a legitimate object of spatial and political analysis. For general readers, it offers a way of seeing the space age that goes beyond the heroic narrative of rockets and astronauts.

Research Question

Why are the world's major space launch sites located where they are, and what does their spatial distribution reveal about the relationship between technology, geography, and power?

Category Framework

Military Origin — Sites originating from Cold War missile programs or active military installations.
Colonial Territory — Sites built on land controlled through colonial or imperial relationships.
National Prestige — Sites built primarily as symbols of national technological ambition.
Commercial — Sites operated primarily by private companies, often inheriting military infrastructure or seeking low-regulation environments.
Peripheral Land — Sites on marginal, depopulated, or indigenous territory chosen because the state could use it without significant political resistance.

Primary Scholarly References

This project draws mainly on Peter Redfield's Space in the Tropics: From Convicts to Rockets in French Guiana (University of California Press, 2000), which provides a framework for reading launch sites as politically and historically meaningful spaces rather than neutral technical facilities.

It also uses Immanuel Wallerstein's world-systems theory, especially The Modern World-System (Academic Press, 1974), to interpret how peripheral territories are repeatedly used to support technological, military, and state-centered ambitions.

Conclusion

What the Map Argues

The Pattern

Across the thirty-three sites examined in this project, a consistent pattern emerges: launch sites are disproportionately located in places that states could use without significant political resistance. Deserts, remote islands, former colonial territories, indigenous lands, depopulated peripheries — these are not incidentally where rockets launch from. They are where rockets launch from because states found them available.

Three Key Findings

1. Military infrastructure became space infrastructure. The majority of major launch sites originated as military missile facilities during the Cold War. The space age did not begin from scratch — it began from a military geography already in place.

2. Colonial and peripheral territories bear a disproportionate share of the burden. Kourou, Alcantara, Woomera, Hammaguir, Luigi Broglio — in each case, a region with less political power hosts infrastructure that serves national or supranational ambitions. The benefits flow outward; the land use remains local.

3. The commercial turn in space has not changed this pattern. Rocket Lab's Mahia Peninsula, Spaceport America, Boca Chica, Spaceport Cornwall — commercial space has largely inherited or replicated the same geographic logic. Private capital seeks the same qualities military planners sought: remoteness, low regulation, and land that can be used.

What This Project Cannot Show

A map can show where launch sites are located. It cannot show labor conditions, environmental consequences, the experiences of displaced communities, or the full complexity of political negotiations. The interpretive notes are starting points, not conclusions.

A Final Thought

The space age is often narrated as humanity's escape from earthly constraints. This project insists that the infrastructure of transcendence is built on earth, in specific places, under specific political conditions. The geography of space is not neutral. It never was.

Data & Method

Transparency, Sources, and Interpretive Choices

Dataset Overview

This project examines 33 major operational or historically significant spaceports selected from a broader list of globally active launch facilities. The dataset was constructed manually. Sites were selected to represent geographic diversity (all inhabited continents), operator diversity (state, military, intergovernmental, commercial), and historical diversity (1946 to 2023). Colonial Territory and Commercial categories were intentionally expanded to reflect their global significance.

Data Sources

Factual data verified through Wikipedia's "List of rocket launch sites" cross-referenced against official agency websites: NASA, ESA, JAXA, KARI, ISRO, CNSA, Roscosmos, Rocket Lab, SpaceX.

Category Construction

The five categories were developed inductively after the initial dataset was assembled — categorization followed observation, not the reverse. The primary theoretical framework comes from Peter Redfield's Space in the Tropics (2000).

Interpretive Notes

All interpretive notes are original analysis by Jeongwoo Jang, drawing on publicly available historical information and the project's analytical framework.
